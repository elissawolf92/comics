What am I looking at?

This is a verbal profile of two newspaper comic strips. One, Garfield, has been telling the story of a lazy fat cat who hates Mondays for thirty-five years. Jim Davis, creator of Garfield, has freely admitted Garfield was "a conscious effort to come up with a good, marketable character". Between $750 million and $1 billion in Garfield merchandise is sold each year.

Calvin & Hobbes ran for just over 10 years. The strip follows a boy and his stuffed tiger, both named for Renaissance-era thinkers. Despite the marketing potential of such a character, cartoonist Bill Watterson refused to allow licensed merchandise because it would be a "detriment to the integrity of the strip". A plush Hobbes doll would answer a question that shouldn't be answered - is Hobbes alive?

Methodology

I ran a text analysis on a full transcript of Calvin & Hobbes and a transcript of three years of Garfield. To perform this analysis, I checked each word in a strip against the Regressive Imagery Dictionary (RID). The RID contains some 3,000 English words divided into two main "processes" - Primary and Secondary. These processes are further divided into categories. The primary process involves more basic concepts like food, sex, and sensation; the Secondary process covers more developed concepts like time, morality, social behavior, emotions, and abstract thought.

From this, I was able to get a percentage breakdown of word categories in each strip. Interpretation of these results was based on a psychological study that found creativity was correlated with higher usage of words in the Primary process category. The top ten subcategories for each comic strip are also visualized. Hovering over a face shows all the words in that subcategory; words in black are found in both the RID and that comic strip's transcript.
